Editorial

This week, the hospitality industry discovered that problems you ignore do not disappear, they compound with interest.

NSW finally ended pokies exemptions that should have expired decades ago. A celebrity chef closed his bakery after two years of harassment that everyone could see but nobody stopped. Restaurants are charging more for fewer visits while real wages fall and inflation climbs. The common thread is not bad luck or external forces. It is the accumulating cost of pretending that temporary solutions are permanent ones, that visibility does not come with risk, and that you can raise prices without changing what you are selling.

The hospitality industry operates like a borrower who keeps refinancing the same debt instead of paying it down. Every temporary fix becomes the new baseline until the next crisis forces another patch. Operators who think they are managing through difficult periods are actually building structural problems that will be more expensive to solve later. The market rewards short-term thinking right up until it demands long-term consequences.

Industry News
This Week's Top Stories
1
NSW Gaming Minister confirms end to pokies shutdown exemptions from March 31, affecting 670+ venues
NSW Gaming Minister David Harris will force more than 670 pubs and clubs to shut their poker machines between 4am and 10am from March 31, 2026, ending decades-old exemptions that allowed 24/7 gaming. Government research shows 70% of gamblers between these hours are high-risk or moderate-risk players.
The decision removes late-night revenue from roughly one-fifth of NSW's venue base. Between June and November 2023, three venues with hardship exemptions ranked in the top ten for gaming profit, suggesting many exemptions no longer match their original criteria.
Venues can still apply for new exemptions under tougher guidelines, with decisions made by the Independent Liquor and Gaming Authority. Watch for appeals and how many venues actually lose their late-night operations versus securing new exemptions.

This is the hospitality equivalent of a landlord finally enforcing the lease after twenty years of looking the other way. The venues operating on exemptions were not breaking rules, they were playing by different rules that everyone knew had outlived their purpose. Now the industry gets to discover which operators built their late-night revenue streams on genuine operational need versus regulatory arbitrage disguised as hardship.
2
Celebrity chef Ed Halmagyi permanently closes Avner's Bakery after years of anti-Semitic harassment
Television personality Ed Halmagyi has permanently closed Avner's Bakery in Sydney's Surry Hills after two years of sustained anti-Semitic harassment. The closure followed threats, vandalism and intimidation that continued even after Sunday's mass shooting at a Hanukkah celebration at Bondi Beach, which killed 15 people. Halmagyi said he could no longer guarantee the safety of staff and customers at the openly Jewish bakery.
Operating as a visibly Jewish business was a deliberate choice for Halmagyi, who believed visibility fostered understanding. The closure intensifies concerns within Sydney's Jewish community about operating safely in public locations, with over 3,700 anti-Semitic incidents documented since October 2023. Halmagyi noted the struggles reflect broader difficulties across Australia's hospitality industry, where cost of living pressures have reduced sales for thousands of businesses.
Halmagyi remains committed to supporting Jewish Australia through other means. He emphasises that responsibility for rising anti-Semitism extends beyond those who commit violence to everyone who refuses to act to prevent it.

üéØ The Game Angle
The celebrity chef closing his bakery is hospitality's version of a canary dying in the coal mine, except the gas leak has been visible for two years and nobody fixed the ventilation. Halmagyi operated Avner's as an openly Jewish business the same way other operators chase Instagram-friendly fitouts or celebrity endorsements. The difference is that visibility as a Jewish business attracted the wrong kind of attention in a market where being the target is more dangerous than being invisible. Every operator who thinks this story is about anti-Semitism and not about what happens when public-facing businesses become symbols is missing the point about risk.
3
Restaurant & Catering Australia forecasts 12% decline in dining frequency but higher spend per head
Restaurant & Catering Australia data shows Australians are dining out 12 per cent less frequently than pre-2020 levels, but spend per head has risen 8 to 15 per cent depending on venue type. Bookings are growing while walk-ins continue to drop, signaling that dining occasions are becoming more intentional rather than impulsive.
This shift forces operators to justify every service moment because customers expect each dining experience to feel worth the higher spend. Venues that deliver consistency and honest value will hold their ground, while those serving average experiences at premium prices will struggle to retain custom.
Operators should focus on menu engineering that splits into efficient core offerings and rotating highlights. Watch booking patterns closely and prepare for customers who plan their visits but demand excellence when they arrive.

The dining industry has accidentally trained customers to behave like luxury shoppers while operators still think like convenience stores. Fewer visits at higher prices means restaurants are competing in the special occasion market whether they know it or not. The operator charging casual prices for casual service is about to discover their customer has already moved on to somewhere that understands the new value equation.
Economics
Economic Pulse
1
RBA forecasts inflation to peak at 3.7% mid-2026 as wages growth remains elevated
The RBA expects underlying inflation to peak at 3.7% by mid-2026, remaining above 3% until early 2027 before returning to the target range midpoint by mid-2028. Wages growth remains elevated at 3.4% annually through December, supporting the bank's assessment that labour market conditions stay tight. The central bank raised rates to 3.85% in February and markets expect further increases if inflation data continues to surprise upward.
Unit labour costs have grown faster than expected due to weak productivity and strong wage growth, though this cost pressure should moderate as productivity picks up. For operators, two rate hikes would add roughly $203 monthly to a $600,000 mortgage. The RBA expects capacity pressures to ease over 2027 as economic activity slows and labour markets gradually soften.
Watch the March quarter inflation data closely. If trimmed mean inflation stays above 3%, a May rate hike becomes near-certain according to major bank forecasts.

üéØ The Game Angle
The RBA is running monetary policy like a restaurant trying to control portion sizes by raising menu prices. Higher rates are meant to cool demand, but operators still need to pay rent, wages, and suppliers regardless of how many covers they lose. The bank assumes that making money more expensive will somehow make labour cheaper, when the real constraint is that both cost pressures operate on different time horizons.
2
Australian real wages fall for second consecutive quarter as cost-of-living pressure mounts
Real wages fell for the second consecutive quarter in December 2025, with nominal wages growing 3.4% annually while private sector wages rose 3.4%. Real wages remain 6% below their Covid-19 peak, tracking at late 2011 levels.
For hospitality operators, this wages plateau hits precisely where it hurts most. The RBA forecasts real wages will still be 6.4% below peak by June 2028, suggesting recovery may take over a decade. Disposable income is not recovering, which means the customer wallet that drives weekend bookings and discretionary dining is shrinking in real terms.
Watch the gap between nominal wage announcements and actual purchasing power. When operators hear about 3.4% wage growth, the reality is customers feel poorer than last year.

Feature Article
The Predictability Paradox: Why Hospitality's Roster Revolution is Missing the Point

As workers demand structured schedules and skills development, operators are discovering that solving the labour shortage requires unlearning everything the industry taught them about flexibility.

The headlines write themselves: hospitality workers across Australia are demanding predictable rosters and clear skills development pathways as the labour shortage deepens. Young workers, particularly millennials and Gen Z, prioritise work-life balance and seek employers who offer flexible work arrangements and better benefits. Award requirements now mandate rosters be published 7 to 14 days in advance, while operators scramble to meet expectations their business models were never designed to deliver.

Here is what the industry is getting wrong. The conversation has become about giving workers what they want instead of understanding why they want it. Average wages in the hospitality sector have traditionally been lower compared to other industries, making it challenging to attract and retain talent, while wages have risen sharply as hotels compete for a dwindling pool of workers, with average hourly earnings now around $22.53 AUD, marking an 8% increase. But wage increases solve nothing if the work itself remains structurally unlivable.

Predictable rosters are not about worker preference. They are about economic survival. When your rent is due on the same date every month but your shifts change weekly, you cannot budget, you cannot plan, and you certainly cannot build a career. The demand for skills development follows the same logic. Workers are not asking for training because they love learning. They are asking because they can see that staying in an industry that treats them as interchangeable parts leads nowhere.

The operators responding to this crisis are dividing into two camps. The first group treats these demands as unreasonable impositions on operational flexibility. They complain about regulatory burden while two-thirds of hotels remain short-staffed. The second group embraces the demands but misses the deeper structural issue. They install rostering software and create training programs while maintaining the same casual-heavy employment model that created the problem.

Both approaches fail because they assume the existing business model can be tweaked rather than rebuilt. Successful strategies focus on staff training and development, equipping employees with better skills and demonstrating a commitment to their growth to build loyalty. But loyalty to what? An industry that still treats full-time employment as a luxury and career progression as an accident?

üí° Key Takeaway: The operators who solve the labour shortage will be those who stop treating predictable rosters and skills development as worker demands and start treating them as business requirements.
üéØ The Game Angle

The hospitality industry is attempting to solve its labour crisis the same way a casino tries to win back problem gamblers: by offering more of the same thing that drove them away in the first place. Operators promise flexible rosters to workers who are desperate for predictability, then act surprised when flexibility becomes a euphemism for chaos. The workers are not being unreasonable. They are being rational actors in an irrational system that asks them to build lives on schedules that change like the weather.

Community
Community Spotlight
Mike McEnearney
Restaurant Chef ¬∑ Melbourne

Mike McEnearney swapped Sydney's restaurant scene for Melbourne's Docklands, opening From Here by Mike at 1 Hotel Melbourne within the past year. The move paid off quickly: a Good Food hat arrived within months, built on a menu that reads like a love letter to Victorian producers. McEnearney sources obsessively local, turning sustainability from marketing copy into menu strategy, and proving that a hotel restaurant can operate with the focus of a neighbourhood bistro.

The timing matters more than the accolades suggest. Hotel restaurants have spent a decade trying to escape the stigma of being convenient rather than compelling, while independent operators have watched food costs climb and reliable suppliers become scarce. McEnearney is solving both problems with the same approach: treating local sourcing as competitive advantage rather than moral obligation. When your suppliers are within driving distance and your menu changes with what actually grows here, you control more variables than the operator chasing imported prestige ingredients.

The real lesson is not about sustainability or hotel dining. It is about constraint as creative force. McEnearney built his reputation in Sydney, then chose to start fresh in a new city with a deliberately narrow brief: make Victorian ingredients the star. The limitation became the point of difference. Other operators might consider what happens when you pick one thing to do exceptionally well, then let that choice dictate everything else. The market rewards clarity more than complexity.

About the Author
Julian Blok
Julian spent 16 years in banking, including Goldman Sachs, before stepping into hospitality in 2014 as the operator of a supplies business serving venues from single-site cafes to national chains. That role gave him something most commentators never get: a supplier's lens. Suppliers do not just see invoices. They see what breaks first, what gets ordered in a panic, and which problems keep showing up in different venues wearing different disguises. Over a decade he watched the same failure patterns repeat across thousands of businesses and started treating them as data, not bad luck.
Today Julian works as an industry consultant helping hospitality operators use behavioural insight and systems-led change, digital and procedural, to build businesses that grow profit, not just revenue. That practitioner's lens is the foundation for The Food Game, his forthcoming book, and for this newsletter: a weekly attempt to name the patterns, question the defaults, and help the people doing the hard work see the game a little more clearly.
